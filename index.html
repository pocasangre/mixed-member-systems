Mixed-member electoral systems

An interactive explainer of how mixed-member proportional (MMP) and mixed-member majoritarian (MMM) systems work. * TEST VERSION - Created with Claude AI *.

Mixed-member systems combine a district (majoritarian) tier with a party-list (proportional) tier. Explore the four key design dimensions below.

The key distinction is whether party-list seats compensate for district outcomes (MMP) or are allocated independently (MMM).

MMP linked
List seats correct distortions from district wins. Parliament may expand with overhang seats.
New Zealand, Germany, Scotland
MMM parallel
Tiers allocated independently. Legislature size stays fixed; dominant parties benefit. Less proportional.
Japan, South Korea, Mexico

Scotland's D'Hondt method (MMP variant)
List votes ÷ (district seats won + 1). The party with the highest quotient gets the next list seat. Size stays fixed — slightly less proportional than New Zealand.

Systems differ in whether voters cast one vote or two — which shapes strategic voting behaviour.

Single vote fused
One vote counts for both district candidate and party. Simpler, but voters can't split preferences.
Example: Mexico (regional pools)
Two votes split
Separate votes for district candidate and party list. Enables split-ticket voting — backing a local candidate strategically while expressing true party preference.
Examples: Germany, New Zealand, Japan
Simulated ballot — Germany style

Hungary — excess vote transfer
Surplus votes for winning candidates flow to their party, disproportionately rewarding dominant parties. Technically MMM despite the linkage.
